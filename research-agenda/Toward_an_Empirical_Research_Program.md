# Toward an Empirical Research Program for Third-Space Extension

**Author:** Rachelle Siemasz  
**Status:** Open research agenda  
**Purpose:** To translate the conceptual model of third-space extension into testable questions, comparative designs, and instrument-development priorities.

## 1. Scope

This agenda outlines future studies that could examine whether, when, and under what conditions recursively coupled human–AI interaction produces measurable changes in cognitive work, interaction quality, human evaluative control, and solitary capacity.

The agenda does not assume that third-space extension has already been empirically established. It identifies candidate hypotheses and study designs that could support, refine, or falsify parts of the framework.

## 2. Foundational Research Questions

1. Under what conditions does generative-AI interaction move beyond ordinary assistance or cognitive offloading?
2. Which interaction features distinguish recursive co-structuring from simple output generation?
3. Which cognitive domains are most susceptible to redistribution?
4. How does explicit corrective feedback affect loop coherence and human evaluative control?
5. What forms of drift are most common in long, recursively coupled sessions?
6. Does repeated interaction strengthen, preserve, or weaken relevant solitary cognitive capacity?
7. How do model architecture, interface design, memory, and context-window behavior affect loop health?
8. Can independent coders reliably identify candidate redistribution events?
9. Do users and observers agree about when cognitive work has become distributed across the interaction?
10. What ethical and design safeguards best preserve agentic sovereignty?

## 3. Program Area A: Construct Clarification and Measurement

### Study A1: Expert Review of the Conceptual Framework

**Goal:** Evaluate the clarity and distinctiveness of third-space extension relative to extended mind, distributed cognition, cognitive offloading, scaffolding, and ordinary collaboration.

**Method:** Structured review by philosophers of mind, cognitive scientists, HCI researchers, and AI-interaction researchers.

**Outputs:** Revised construct definitions, boundary cases, and candidate exclusion criteria.

### Study A2: Annotation Schema Pilot

**Goal:** Test whether coders can apply the Interaction Annotation Schema consistently.

**Method:** Two or more coders annotate a shared set of human–AI transcripts across writing, programming, and research tasks.

**Measures:** Inter-rater agreement, code confusion, missing categories, annotation time, coder confidence.

### Study A3: Loop Health Index Content Validation

**Goal:** Assess whether the proposed dimensions adequately represent loop quality.

**Method:** Expert ratings of relevance, clarity, redundancy, and missing dimensions.

**Candidate dimensions:** goal continuity, thread preservation, transformative contribution, human evaluative control, drift burden, correction responsiveness, recursive reintegration, and agentic sovereignty.

### Study A4: Distinguishing Assistance from Redistribution

**Goal:** Test whether coders can distinguish ordinary assistance, scaffolding, and candidate redistribution.

**Method:** Present blinded transcript segments representing search-like use, editing support, iterative collaboration, and deep recursive coupling.

**Primary question:** Do the protocol criteria improve classification consistency?

## 4. Program Area B: Controlled Interaction Studies

### Study B1: Search Engine versus Generative AI

**Design:** Participants complete matched synthesis tasks using either a search engine or a generative AI system.

**Outcomes:** thread integration, number of conceptual revisions, novelty, time, error rate, and post-task explanation quality.

### Study B2: One-Shot versus Recursive Interaction

**Design:** Compare one-shot prompting with a multi-cycle externalization–restructuring–correction–reintegration protocol.

**Outcomes:** output quality, conceptual depth, correction frequency, goal continuity, and user-reported co-processing.

### Study B3: Radical Candor Intervention

**Design:** Randomize participants to ordinary prompting instructions or explicit radical-candor training.

**Outcomes:** drift detection, factual correction, human evaluative control, correction responsiveness, and final-project integrity.

### Study B4: Induced Drift Challenge

**Design:** Introduce controlled model deviations, unsupported assumptions, or subtle reframing.

**Outcomes:** detection latency, correction specificity, boundary restoration, and persistence of the induced frame.

### Study B5: Context Continuity Manipulation

**Design:** Compare full-context interaction, truncated context, summary memory, and no-memory conditions.

**Outcomes:** thread preservation, goal continuity, repeated errors, and reintegration quality.

### Study B6: Model Architecture Comparison

**Design:** Run matched tasks across several model families using standardized interaction protocols.

**Outcomes:** loop-health profiles, drift types, correction responsiveness, and transformative contribution.

## 5. Program Area C: Domain-Specific Studies

### Study C1: Longitudinal Writers

Track writers using recursive AI interaction across ideation, drafting, revision, and finalization.

**Questions:** Where does synthesis occur? Does authorial voice remain stable? How does correction affect output ownership?

### Study C2: Programmers and Debugging

Observe developers working with AI across bug diagnosis, code generation, testing, and revision.

**Questions:** Does the interaction redistribute multi-threaded problem tracking? How often does human correction restore technical coherence?

### Study C3: Researchers and Literature Synthesis

Compare solitary and AI-supported synthesis of complex literatures.

**Questions:** Does recursive interaction improve cross-domain integration while preserving source accuracy and claim boundaries?

### Study C4: Designers and Artists

Study visual designers, musicians, and artists using AI during concept development.

**Questions:** How do novelty, authorship, selection, and rejection operate across the loop?

### Study C5: Clinicians in Non-Diagnostic Tasks

Examine documentation, education, and administrative reasoning rather than autonomous diagnosis.

**Questions:** Does AI support cognitive bandwidth without displacing professional judgment?

### Study C6: Educators and Curriculum Design

Study lesson planning, adaptation, and assessment design.

**Questions:** When does the system become a temporary locus of multi-threaded pedagogical reasoning?

### Study C7: High-Complexity Decision Support

Use simulated policy, operations, or planning tasks.

**Questions:** Does recursive coupling improve option generation while increasing framing risk?

## 6. Program Area D: Longitudinal and Capacity Effects

### Study D1: Solitary Capacity Retention

Measure participants before, during, and after a period of intensive AI-supported work.

**Outcomes:** unaided synthesis, working-memory performance within the target domain, task confidence, and transfer.

### Study D2: Skill Development versus Dependency

Compare novice and expert users over time.

**Questions:** Does the same protocol scaffold learning for novices while preserving expert judgment? Under what conditions does passive reliance emerge?

### Study D3: Cumulative Calibration

Track whether repeated correction improves model responsiveness within a project and whether users become better at diagnosing drift.

### Study D4: Cross-Session Pattern Tracking

Study whether persistent records, memory systems, or external logs improve meta-cognitive pattern recognition across sessions.

### Study D5: Disengagement and Recovery

Examine what happens when users temporarily stop interacting with the system.

**Questions:** Which cognitive functions return immediately to solitary performance? Which show short-term disruption or improvement?

## 7. Program Area E: Phenomenology and Mixed Methods

### Study E1: Think-Aloud Protocols

Collect concurrent verbal reports during recursive interaction.

**Goal:** Identify where users experience externalization, surprise, correction, and reintegration.

### Study E2: Stimulated Recall Interviews

Replay transcript segments and ask participants to identify what changed in their thinking.

### Study E3: Experience Sampling

Prompt participants during long projects to report cognitive bandwidth, ownership, clarity, dependency, and drift.

### Study E4: Comparative Phenomenology

Compare experienced and novice users, different task domains, and different model architectures.

### Study E5: First-Person and Third-Person Agreement

Compare user reports with independent transcript coding.

**Question:** Do felt co-processing and observable recursive integration converge?

## 8. Program Area F: Neurocognitive and Behavioral Extensions

These studies are higher-cost and should follow construct clarification.

### Study F1: EEG During Recursive Coupling

Compare neural signatures across solitary ideation, search use, one-shot AI use, and recursive interaction.

### Study F2: Eye-Tracking and Attention Allocation

Measure how users distribute attention between their own drafts, model output, source material, and corrections.

### Study F3: Cognitive Load Measurement

Use validated workload measures and secondary-task paradigms to test whether recursive interaction reduces local load while increasing system-level complexity.

### Study F4: Memory for Accepted versus Rejected Material

Test whether users remember the provenance and justification of model-generated ideas after reintegration.

### Study F5: Error Ownership and Source Monitoring

Examine whether users can identify which claims originated with them, the model, or external evidence.

## 9. Program Area G: Interface and Design Research

### Study G1: Visible Loop-State Interface

Create interfaces that explicitly mark externalization, model transformation, correction, and reintegration phases.

### Study G2: Drift Alerts

Test interface indicators for goal deviation, contradiction, or unaddressed correction.

### Study G3: Correction Memory

Evaluate whether systems that visibly retain prior corrections reduce recurrence and improve trust calibration.

### Study G4: Thread Maps

Test visual maps of conceptual threads across long sessions.

### Study G5: Sovereignty-Preserving Defaults

Compare interfaces that encourage critique and source checking with interfaces optimized primarily for rapid acceptance.

### Study G6: Friction as a Safeguard

Investigate whether carefully placed pauses, evidence prompts, or acceptance checks improve evaluation without making the system unusable.

## 10. Program Area H: Ethical and Social Questions

### Study H1: Unequal Capacity for Boundary Maintenance

Examine whether differences in expertise, literacy, confidence, disability, or domain knowledge affect the ability to detect and correct drift.

### Study H2: Institutional Incentives

Study whether workplaces reward rapid output in ways that discourage human evaluation and correction.

### Study H3: Hidden Norm Substitution

Analyze how model defaults may replace user, professional, cultural, or disciplinary standards.

### Study H4: Authorship and Credit

Investigate how recursive co-structuring complicates conventional accounts of authorship without erasing human goal authorship and evaluative authority.

### Study H5: Privacy and Cognitive Intimacy

Examine the risks of externalizing incomplete thoughts, private associations, and developing conceptual structures into commercial systems.

### Study H6: Accessibility and Cognitive Support

Study whether third-space protocols provide meaningful support for users with executive-function, language, memory, or motor constraints while avoiding paternalistic assumptions.

## 11. Priority Sequence

A practical development sequence is:

1. expert construct review
2. annotation-schema pilot
3. loop-health content validation
4. small controlled comparison of one-shot and recursive interaction
5. radical-candor intervention study
6. domain-specific longitudinal studies
7. cross-model replication
8. solitary-capacity and transfer studies
9. interface experiments
10. neurocognitive extensions

## 12. Open Hypotheses

The following hypotheses are deliberately provisional:

- Recursive interaction will produce more transformative contribution than one-shot use, but also more opportunities for drift.
- Radical-candor training will increase correction specificity, correction responsiveness, and human evaluative control.
- High thread preservation and recursive reintegration will better distinguish candidate redistribution from simple frequency or duration of AI use.
- Experienced users will show stronger agentic sovereignty but may also externalize more cognitive work.
- Memory and long context will improve continuity while potentially increasing unnoticed norm substitution.
- High loop health will predict better task integrity more reliably than user satisfaction alone.
- Solitary capacity outcomes will vary by task, user expertise, and whether the interaction requires active evaluation.

## 13. Data and Reporting Recommendations

Future studies should report:

- model and interface version
- context and memory settings
- task domain
- participant experience level
- full interaction protocol
- correction opportunities
- transcript inclusion and exclusion rules
- annotation procedure
- coder disagreement
- evidence for claims of redistribution
- negative and null findings

Where possible, de-identified transcripts, annotation guides, and analysis code should be shared.

## 14. Falsification Conditions

The framework should be revised or narrowed if research finds that:

- candidate redistribution cannot be reliably distinguished from ordinary assistance
- recursive interaction adds no explanatory or predictive value beyond task duration or tool frequency
- radical candor does not improve correction, coherence, or human evaluative control
- phenomenological reports consistently fail to correspond with observable interaction patterns
- purported benefits disappear when output quality and user expertise are controlled
- repeated coupling reliably weakens relevant solitary capacity without compensating gains
