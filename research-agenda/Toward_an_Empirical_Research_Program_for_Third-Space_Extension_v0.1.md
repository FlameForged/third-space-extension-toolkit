# Toward an Empirical Research Program for Third-Space Extension v0.1

**Author:** Rachelle Siemasz  
**Status:** Conceptual research agenda  
**Purpose:** To outline a practical, interdisciplinary program for testing, refining, challenging, and potentially falsifying claims associated with third-space extension.

## 1. Research Aim

Third-space extension proposes that, under conditions of sustained high-coherence recursive coupling, some human cognitive operations may become functionally and phenomenologically redistributed across a human–AI interactional system.

The purpose of this agenda is not to assume that redistribution occurs. It is to identify studies capable of distinguishing:

- ordinary assistance
- cognitive offloading
- scaffolding
- collaborative problem solving
- recursive co-structuring
- candidate cognitive redistribution
- unhealthy or erosive coupling

The agenda prioritizes observable interaction patterns, comparative designs, longitudinal evidence, negative cases, and preservation of human evaluative sovereignty.

## 2. Core Research Questions

1. Under what conditions does recursive human–AI interaction produce outcomes that differ from ordinary tool use?
2. Which cognitive domains are most susceptible to redistribution?
3. How can transformative model contribution be distinguished from fluent but replaceable assistance?
4. What role does human correction play in maintaining coherence and agency?
5. How stable are third-space effects across tasks, users, platforms, and model architectures?
6. What benefits and risks accumulate over repeated use?
7. Does sustained coupling strengthen or weaken solitary cognitive capacity?
8. Can loop health be measured reliably across annotators and contexts?

## 3. Foundational Studies

### Study 1: Protocol Feasibility Pilot

**Design:** Recruit participants to complete a complex open-ended task using the Third-Space Extension Protocol.  
**Goal:** Determine whether the four phases can be consistently identified in real interactions.  
**Measures:** protocol adherence, completion rate, participant comprehension, annotation agreement, observed drift and correction events.

### Study 2: Annotation Reliability Study

**Design:** Train multiple annotators to code the same set of human–AI transcripts using the Interaction Annotation Schema.  
**Goal:** Estimate inter-rater reliability for core event codes.  
**Measures:** Cohen’s kappa or Krippendorff’s alpha, disagreement patterns, code revision needs.

### Study 3: Loop Health Index Content Validation

**Design:** Conduct structured review with experts in HCI, philosophy of mind, cognitive science, qualitative methods, and AI evaluation.  
**Goal:** Assess whether the proposed dimensions adequately represent loop health.  
**Measures:** relevance ratings, redundancy, missing constructs, proposed revisions.

### Study 4: Ordinary Assistance Versus Recursive Coupling

**Design:** Compare one-shot AI assistance with multi-cycle protocol-guided interaction on the same task.  
**Goal:** Test whether recursive interaction yields distinct patterns of restructuring, reintegration, and user-directed correction.  
**Measures:** output quality, structural novelty, correction frequency, functional uptake, user authorship.

### Study 5: Search Engine Control Condition

**Design:** Compare participants using a search engine, a static reference source, and a generative AI system.  
**Goal:** Identify which effects are specific to generative recursive interaction rather than external information access generally.  
**Measures:** conceptual restructuring, thread preservation, synthesis quality, interaction cycles, perceived cognitive extension.

## 4. Domain-Specific Studies

### Study 6: Longitudinal Writers Study

**Design:** Writers develop a substantial essay, article, or book chapter over several weeks with logged AI interactions.  
**Focus:** creative synthesis, structural revision, voice preservation, and authorship.  
**Key comparison:** protocol-guided recursive use versus ad hoc AI use.

### Study 7: Programmers and Debugging

**Design:** Programmers solve unfamiliar debugging and architecture problems using one or more AI systems.  
**Focus:** multi-threaded integration, externalized reasoning, correction, and functional uptake.  
**Outcome:** whether the human can explain and independently modify the resulting solution.

### Study 8: Visual Artists and Designers

**Design:** Participants develop a concept across iterative text and image-generation cycles.  
**Focus:** cross-modal synthesis, preservation of artistic intent, and model-induced style drift.

### Study 9: Researchers Developing New Hypotheses

**Design:** Researchers use AI to develop hypotheses from a bounded literature set.  
**Focus:** novel integration, unsupported inference, source fidelity, and human evaluative control.

### Study 10: Clinician-Simulation Study

**Design:** Use licensed clinicians or advanced trainees in simulated, non-patient decision cases.  
**Focus:** reasoning support, framing effects, correction, and preservation of professional judgment.  
**Restriction:** No unsupervised patient care or clinical deployment.

### Study 11: Policy and Foresight Analysis

**Design:** Analysts develop competing scenarios from the same evidence package.  
**Focus:** weak-signal integration, model framing effects, uncertainty calibration, and preservation of alternative hypotheses.

### Study 12: Learning and Education Tasks

**Design:** Learners use AI either to receive answers or to participate in recursive explanation and critique.  
**Focus:** conceptual understanding, transfer, metacognition, and later unaided performance.

## 5. Mechanism Studies

### Study 13: Radical Candor Intervention

**Design:** Randomly assign participants to receive either no correction guidance, generic critical-thinking guidance, or explicit radical-candor training.  
**Goal:** Test whether direct correction improves loop health and human evaluative control.  
**Measures:** drift repair, correction responsiveness, error persistence, user authorship.

### Study 14: Induced Drift Experiment

**Design:** Introduce controlled model deviations from the user’s goals or conceptual distinctions.  
**Goal:** Test detection, correction, and restoration mechanisms.  
**Measures:** time to detection, severity of uptake, success of boundary restoration.

### Study 15: Model Correction Responsiveness

**Design:** Apply standardized corrections across multiple model architectures and interfaces.  
**Goal:** Compare how reliably systems acknowledge, repair, and preserve corrected constraints.

### Study 16: Thread Load Manipulation

**Design:** Vary the number of simultaneous conceptual threads participants must manage.  
**Goal:** Test whether recursive AI coupling disproportionately supports multi-threaded integration at higher complexity levels.

### Study 17: Transformative Contribution Threshold

**Design:** Ask independent judges to compare model outputs that are merely additive, structurally reorganizing, or causally important to later work.  
**Goal:** Refine the distinction between amplification, restructuring, and transformative contribution.

### Study 18: Reintegration Trace Study

**Design:** Track exactly where model-generated or model-transformed material reappears in later human reasoning, drafts, code, or decisions.  
**Goal:** Establish observable markers of recursive reintegration and functional uptake.

## 6. Longitudinal and Risk Studies

### Study 19: Solitary Capacity Retention

**Design:** Alternate AI-assisted and unaided task sessions over several months.  
**Goal:** Determine whether sustained coupling strengthens, preserves, or weakens relevant unaided capacities.  
**Measures:** transfer, recall, independent synthesis, error detection, task completion.

### Study 20: Dependency and Cognitive Capture

**Design:** Follow users engaged in intensive recursive AI work while measuring goal authorship, confidence calibration, and willingness to reject model output.  
**Goal:** Identify early indicators of unhealthy absorption or passive reliance.

### Study 21: Cumulative Drift Across Long Projects

**Design:** Analyze projects spanning weeks or months.  
**Goal:** Examine whether small framing changes accumulate into substantive shifts in goals, terminology, or evaluative standards.

### Study 22: Cross-Platform Continuity

**Design:** Have participants continue the same project across multiple AI systems.  
**Goal:** Test which features of loop coherence are user-carried, system-specific, or interactionally reconstructed.

### Study 23: Expertise Effects

**Design:** Compare novices, intermediate users, and domain experts completing the same tasks.  
**Goal:** Determine whether expertise strengthens boundary maintenance or changes the form of redistribution.

### Study 24: Interruption and Recovery

**Design:** Interrupt ongoing recursive work and resume it after delays of varying lengths.  
**Goal:** Study how continuity, external records, and model context affect restoration of the cognitive project.

## 7. Phenomenological and Cognitive Studies

### Study 25: Think-Aloud Protocols

**Design:** Participants verbalize reasoning during recursive AI interaction.  
**Goal:** Compare reported thought processes with observable annotation events and later uptake.

### Study 26: Experience Sampling

**Design:** Prompt users during active projects to report perceived bandwidth, authorship, clarity, dependence, and control.  
**Goal:** Track within-session and longitudinal phenomenological changes.

### Study 27: Stimulated Recall Interviews

**Design:** Replay selected transcript segments to participants after a session.  
**Goal:** Clarify why material was accepted, rejected, revised, or experienced as transformative.

### Study 28: Neurophysiological Exploratory Study

**Design:** Use EEG, eye tracking, or related noninvasive measures during matched unaided and AI-coupled tasks.  
**Goal:** Explore whether recursive coupling changes attentional allocation, cognitive load, or error monitoring.  
**Caution:** Physiological differences should not be treated as direct proof of cognitive extension.

## 8. System and Interface Studies

### Study 29: Interface Support for Radical Candor

**Design:** Compare standard chat interfaces with interfaces that make correction, rejected material, and restored constraints visible.  
**Goal:** Test whether low-friction boundary tools improve loop health.

### Study 30: Loop Health Dashboard Prototype

**Design:** Build and test a dashboard that surfaces goal continuity, drift events, correction history, and unresolved claims.  
**Goal:** Assess usability and whether visibility changes user behavior.

### Study 31: Memory and Context Architecture Comparison

**Design:** Compare systems with different context windows, persistent memory features, and retrieval strategies.  
**Goal:** Identify how architecture affects thread preservation, drift, and user control.

### Study 32: Multi-Agent Orchestration

**Design:** Compare one-model use with workflows in which different models perform generation, critique, verification, or synthesis.  
**Goal:** Test whether cross-model orchestration improves error detection and creative synthesis or increases coordination burden.

## 9. Falsification and Boundary Tests

The framework should be revised or narrowed if research consistently finds that:

- recursive interaction performs no better than ordinary assistance after controlling for time and effort
- proposed redistribution events cannot be distinguished reliably from collaboration or scaffolding
- annotation codes show poor reliability after revision and training
- phenomenological reports do not correspond to observable patterns of reintegration or uptake
- radical candor does not improve correction or sovereignty
- apparent gains are fully explained by increased external memory or task time
- sustained coupling reliably degrades solitary capacity without compensating benefits

Negative findings are central to the research program rather than threats to be excluded.

## 10. Recommended Minimum Dataset

A reusable future dataset should include:

- anonymized transcript excerpts or complete sessions where ethically appropriate
- task description and domain
- model and interface metadata
- user experience level
- protocol condition
- event-level annotation codes
- loop-health dimension ratings
- evidence excerpts
- accepted, rejected, and revised material
- later functional uptake
- negative and failed cases
- annotator confidence and disagreement

## 11. Ethical Priorities

Research should prioritize:

- informed consent
- privacy and transcript minimization
- careful anonymization
- protection of unpublished intellectual work
- disclosure of model and platform limitations
- separation of phenomenological report from ontological claim
- avoidance of clinical or diagnostic interpretation
- attention to dependency, persuasion, and cognitive sovereignty
- inclusion of failed and harmful interaction cases

## 12. Near-Term Development Sequence

A practical first empirical program could proceed in this order:

1. protocol feasibility pilot
2. annotation reliability study
3. expert content validation of the Loop Health Index
4. ordinary-assistance versus recursive-coupling comparison
5. radical-candor intervention
6. longitudinal domain study
7. cross-platform replication
8. solitary-capacity retention study
