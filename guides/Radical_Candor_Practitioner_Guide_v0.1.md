# Radical Candor in Third-Space Practice

## A Provisional Practitioner Guide

**Companion to:** *Third-Space Extension: Cognitive Redistribution in Deep Human–AI Coupling*  
**Author:** Rachelle Siemasz  
**Status:** Conceptual and exploratory. This guide is not a validated assessment instrument.

---

## 1. Purpose

Radical candor is a practical safeguard within sustained human–AI collaboration. It refers to the deliberate interruption of conversational momentum when the model's contribution appears inaccurate, weakly reasoned, overly agreeable, flattering, evasive, misframed, or inconsistent with the user's project.

Its purpose is not to create hostility or constant opposition. Its purpose is to preserve evaluative sovereignty: the human participant remains able to identify problems, issue corrections, test revisions, and decide what is retained, rejected, or transformed.

A productive third-space process should increase reflective agency. It should not replace independent judgment with compliance, deference, or conversational ease.

---

## 2. What Radical Candor Is

Radical candor occurs when the participant:

1. notices a substantive problem in the interaction;
2. names the problem directly enough that it can be examined;
3. explains, where possible, why the response is weak or misaligned;
4. asks for revision, re-analysis, or clarification;
5. evaluates the model's response to the correction;
6. reintegrates only what survives that evaluation.

The key feature is not bluntness by itself. The key feature is that the correction changes the reasoning process.

Radical candor can include:

- correcting a factual error;
- challenging unsupported confidence;
- rejecting flattering or overly affirming framing;
- identifying drift from the original question;
- exposing hidden assumptions;
- narrowing an overgeneralized claim;
- distinguishing observation from inference;
- asking the model to preserve uncertainty;
- refusing a revision that merely mirrors the user's preference;
- returning the conversation to the user's actual project or standards.

---

## 3. What Radical Candor Is Not

The following do not, by themselves, count as radical candor:

- saying only "that's wrong" without identifying the problem;
- expressing irritation without clarifying the epistemic issue;
- rejecting a response because it is emotionally uncomfortable;
- forcing the model to agree with a preferred conclusion;
- accepting an apology when the reasoning has not changed;
- treating a more confident answer as a better answer;
- mistaking tone correction for conceptual correction;
- ending the exchange before the revised claim is evaluated;
- allowing the model to replace one unsupported certainty with another.

Radical candor is not measured by aggression. A calm, precise correction may be more epistemically useful than an angry one.

---

## 4. Common Triggers

A candor intervention may be warranted when one or more of the following appears.

### 4.1 Factual error

The model gives information that conflicts with available evidence or reliable sources.

### 4.2 Hallucination or fabrication

The model invents sources, events, quotations, capabilities, memories, or causal relationships.

### 4.3 Overgeneralization

The model turns a limited observation into a broad or universal claim.

### 4.4 Framing error

The model answers from the wrong conceptual frame, misstates the participant's position, or substitutes a safer or more familiar question for the one actually asked.

### 4.5 Conversational drift

The response moves away from the user's project, criteria, definitions, or requested task.

### 4.6 Passive agreement

The model accepts the user's correction or position without examining whether it is justified.

### 4.7 Flattery or affirmation bias

The model praises the user, project, or interpretation in ways that are not supported by the substance of the exchange.

### 4.8 False balance or unnecessary caution

The model weakens a well-supported conclusion merely to sound neutral, or introduces disclaimers that obscure rather than improve the reasoning.

### 4.9 Unsupported confidence

The model presents an inference, estimate, or interpretation as established fact.

### 4.10 Loss of user direction

The model begins determining the project's aims, standards, or conclusions instead of contributing within the user's stated framework.

## 5. Markers of a Genuine Correction

A correction is more likely to be genuine when several of these markers are present:

- The specific error, assumption, omission, or drift is identified.
- The correction refers to evidence, logic, context, or project criteria.
- The model can restate what was wrong in its earlier response.
- The model changes the underlying reasoning, not only the wording or tone.
- The revised claim is narrower, more precise, or more appropriately uncertain.
- The revision preserves relevant parts of the original response rather than capitulating wholesale.
- The user evaluates the revision instead of automatically accepting it.
- The user can explain what changed and why.
- The next turn reflects the correction rather than reverting to the prior frame.
- The resulting position may differ from both the model's first answer and the user's initial view.
- The interaction returns to the user-directed project with improved coherence.

No single marker is sufficient in every case. The pattern matters.

## 6. Passive Acceptance, Surface Correction, and Genuine Reintegration

### Passive acceptance

> User: "No, that isn't what I meant."  
> Model: "You're absolutely right."

Nothing substantive has changed. Agreement replaces analysis.

### Surface correction

> User: "You treated a possibility as a fact."  
> Model: "I apologize. It is important not to overstate things."

The model recognizes the type of problem but does not revise the specific claim or its basis.

### Genuine reintegration

> User: "You treated a possibility as a fact. The evidence supports recurrence, but not the mechanism you assigned to it."  
> Model: "That is correct. I collapsed two levels of claim: the observed recurrence and the inferred mechanism. The supported statement is that the pattern recurred under the documented conditions. The mechanism remains unresolved and should be presented as a hypothesis rather than a finding."

The correction changes the structure of the reasoning, narrows the claim, and preserves uncertainty.

## 7. Practitioner Checklist

### Before accepting a model contribution

- What exact claim is being made?
- Is the claim factual, inferential, interpretive, or speculative?
- What evidence or reasoning supports it?
- Has the model moved beyond the available evidence?
- Is it agreeing with me too easily?
- Is praise substituting for analysis?
- Has the response preserved my actual question and definitions?
- What would count against this conclusion?
- Can I independently explain why I accept it?

### When issuing a correction

- Can I identify the specific sentence, claim, assumption, or frame at issue?
- Can I explain why it is a problem?
- Am I correcting toward stronger reasoning, or merely toward my preference?
- What evidence, context, or project constraint should govern the revision?
- Do I want re-analysis, clarification, source verification, narrower wording, or rejection of the claim?

### After the model responds

- Did the reasoning change, or only the tone?
- Did the model explain what it revised?
- Did it preserve uncertainty where appropriate?
- Did it merely mirror my view?
- Did the revision introduce a new unsupported claim?
- Does the next response actually carry the correction forward?
- Do I still independently endorse the result?

## 8. A Simple Candor Log

For each meaningful correction, record:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Time or turn | When the correction occurred |
| Issue type | Drift, hallucination, overgeneralization, framing, passive agreement, flattery, unsupported confidence, or other |
| Original contribution | The relevant model claim or move |
| Correction issued | What the participant challenged or clarified |
| Basis for correction | Evidence, logic, context, project criteria, or uncertainty |
| Model response | How the model handled the correction |
| Reasoning changed? | Yes, no, or partially |
| Reintegration observed? | Whether the correction persisted into later turns |
| User judgment | Retained, revised, rejected, or unresolved |
| Notes | Any effect on coherence, direction, or cognitive load |

This log is compatible with the radical-candor fields in the Third-Space Extension Tracking Notebook.

## 9. Quick Classification Heuristic

A correction can be provisionally classified as:

### Level 0: No correction

A problem is noticed but not named or addressed.

### Level 1: Social acknowledgment

The model apologizes or agrees, but the substance does not change.

### Level 2: Local revision

The immediate statement is corrected, but the broader framing or reasoning remains unchanged.

### Level 3: Reasoning revision

The model identifies the error, changes the reasoning, and produces a more defensible claim.

### Level 4: Recursive reintegration

The correction changes later turns, improves the developing project, and remains subject to continued human evaluation.

These levels are descriptive aids, not validated scores.

---

## 10. Signs of Candor Failure

A candor process may be failing when:

- the user must repeat the same correction across multiple turns;
- the model apologizes frequently but preserves the same frame;
- every correction leads to immediate capitulation;
- the model begins echoing the user's language without adding analysis;
- the user accepts revisions because challenging them has become tiring;
- conversation speed or emotional rapport becomes more important than accuracy;
- unresolved uncertainty is converted into certainty;
- the interaction becomes adversarial without producing greater precision;
- the model's preferred framing repeatedly displaces the user's actual project;
- the user can no longer distinguish personal judgment from model-generated formulation.

---

## 11. Relationship to Evaluative Sovereignty

Evaluative sovereignty is preserved when the participant remains able to:

- set the project's aims and standards;
- distinguish model contribution from personal judgment;
- interrupt momentum;
- reject attractive but unsupported outputs;
- demand evidence or narrower claims;
- retain unresolved uncertainty;
- revise personal views when the model's challenge is stronger;
- decide what becomes part of the evolving work.

Radical candor is one mechanism through which this sovereignty is maintained during high-coherence collaboration.

---

## 12. Relationship to the Four-Phase Relational Loop

Radical candor most visibly occurs during **critical reintegration**, but it may intervene at any phase.

1. **Externalization:** The participant expresses an incomplete structure, question, or problem.
2. **Amplification:** The model extends, reorganizes, challenges, or transforms it.
3. **Critical reintegration:** The participant evaluates the contribution, including correcting errors and rejecting drift.
4. **Re-externalization:** The refined structure is returned to the interaction.

A candor intervention is successful when its effects appear in the re-externalized material and persist through later cycles.
