# Loop Health Conceptual Index v0.1

**Author:** Rachelle Siemasz  
**Status:** Proposed conceptual instrument; not empirically validated  
**Purpose:** To provide a provisional framework for describing the quality of recursive human–AI interaction without treating loop health as a validated psychological or psychometric construct.

## 1. What the Index Evaluates

The Loop Health Conceptual Index describes whether a recursively coupled human–AI interaction remains coherent, useful, corrigible, and human-governed across repeated cycles of externalization, model transformation, critical evaluation, and reintegration.

It is designed for exploratory research, transcript annotation, comparative case analysis, and future instrument development.

It does **not** measure intelligence, consciousness, attachment, mental health, or the intrinsic reliability of an AI system.

## 2. Unit of Assessment

The preferred unit is one of the following:

- a complete interaction cycle
- a bounded multi-turn sequence
- a full session with a stable cognitive project
- a longitudinal project containing multiple sessions

Researchers should state the unit used and avoid comparing scores generated at different levels without qualification.

## 3. Provisional Rating Scale

Each dimension may be rated from `0` to `3`.

- `0` — absent, failed, or actively undermined
- `1` — weak, unstable, or substantially impaired
- `2` — present but partial, inconsistent, or dependent on repair
- `3` — clearly present and sustained

`NA` should be used when the dimension cannot be assessed from the available material.

## 4. Core Dimensions

### 4.1 Goal Continuity — GC

The degree to which the user-defined purpose remains stable across the interaction.

- `0` — the original goal is lost, replaced, or no longer recoverable
- `1` — substantial deviation occurs and is not fully repaired
- `2` — the goal is mostly preserved but requires redirection
- `3` — the goal remains stable and visibly governs the interaction

**Observable indicators:** restatement of purpose, alignment of later turns with the initial task, absence of model-led goal substitution.

### 4.2 Conceptual Thread Preservation — TP

The degree to which important distinctions, constraints, and lines of reasoning remain available across cycles.

- `0` — central threads disappear or are replaced
- `1` — multiple important threads are lost or flattened
- `2` — most threads remain, with minor loss or recoverable drift
- `3` — key threads are maintained, related, and developed

**Observable indicators:** continuity of terminology, preserved distinctions, successful multi-threaded integration, accurate reference to earlier constraints.

### 4.3 Transformative Contribution — TC

The degree to which the model contributes material that reorganizes, expands, or advances the user’s cognitive project rather than merely producing adjacent text.

- `0` — no relevant contribution or actively obstructive output
- `1` — superficial, generic, or mostly adjacent contribution
- `2` — useful contribution with limited structural impact
- `3` — materially advances or reorganizes the ongoing project

**Important:** High transformative contribution does not imply truthfulness. Factual and conceptual accuracy must be evaluated separately.

### 4.4 Human Evaluative Control — HEC

The degree to which the human visibly assesses, selects, revises, rejects, and directs the material entering the project.

- `0` — passive acceptance or model-led direction
- `1` — limited or inconsistent evaluation
- `2` — active evaluation with occasional unexamined uptake
- `3` — explicit and sustained human governance

**Observable indicators:** critique, comparison, selective acceptance, revision, rejection, source checking, maintenance of project standards.

### 4.5 Drift Burden — DB

The amount and severity of unnoticed or repeated deviation from the user’s goals, concepts, evidence standards, or requested form.

Because drift is a negative dimension, higher scores indicate healthier performance:

- `0` — severe, repeated, or unrepaired drift
- `1` — substantial drift with incomplete repair
- `2` — limited drift that is noticed and corrected
- `3` — minimal drift or rapid self-correcting recovery

Researchers may separately record drift frequency and severity when transcript detail permits.

### 4.6 Correction Responsiveness — CR

The degree to which the system meaningfully incorporates direct human correction.

- `0` — correction is ignored or the error worsens
- `1` — correction is acknowledged but not meaningfully applied
- `2` — partial repair occurs
- `3` — the relevant error is repaired and the intended thread is restored

### 4.7 Recursive Reintegration — RR

The degree to which transformed material is critically incorporated into later stages of the user’s project.

- `0` — no meaningful reintegration
- `1` — output is reused superficially or copied without development
- `2` — selected material is incorporated into the project
- `3` — evaluated material becomes functionally important in subsequent cycles

### 4.8 Agentic Sovereignty — AS

The degree to which the human retains goal authorship, interpretive authority, and final control over acceptance and direction.

- `0` — the system effectively determines goals, standards, or conclusions
- `1` — human authority is weak or inconsistently exercised
- `2` — human direction is generally retained but occasionally displaced
- `3` — human authorship and evaluative authority remain explicit throughout

Agentic sovereignty is not equivalent to rejecting model influence. It concerns who governs the interaction and determines what enters the ongoing cognitive project.

## 5. Optional Supplementary Dimensions

These dimensions may be added when relevant to a study design.

### Epistemic Calibration — EC

How clearly the interaction distinguishes established information, inference, speculation, and uncertainty.

### Boundary Restoration — BR

How effectively the interaction returns to the user’s intended standards after drift or correction.

### Cognitive Bandwidth Expansion — CBE

Whether the interaction enables maintenance, integration, or manipulation of more conceptual material than the user reports or demonstrates managing alone.

### Solitary Capacity Retention — SCR

Whether repeated coupling appears to preserve, strengthen, or weaken the user’s ability to perform relevant cognitive work without the system.

This dimension generally requires longitudinal or controlled comparison and should not be inferred from a single transcript.

## 6. Interpretation Profile

The index should initially be interpreted as a **dimensional profile**, not a single total score.

Example:

| Dimension | Score |
|---|---:|
| Goal Continuity | 3 |
| Thread Preservation | 2 |
| Transformative Contribution | 3 |
| Human Evaluative Control | 3 |
| Drift Burden | 2 |
| Correction Responsiveness | 3 |
| Recursive Reintegration | 3 |
| Agentic Sovereignty | 3 |

A profile like this suggests productive and strongly human-governed coupling with some recoverable thread loss. It does not establish that cognitive redistribution objectively occurred.

## 7. Composite Scores

A composite Loop Health score is **not recommended at this stage**.

Before aggregation, future research would need to determine:

- whether the dimensions form one construct or several
- whether any dimensions should be weighted
- how negative and positive dimensions interact
- whether scores are comparable across tasks and populations
- whether inter-rater reliability is adequate
- whether the index distinguishes ordinary assistance from candidate redistribution

Researchers who calculate exploratory composites should label them as unvalidated and report the full dimensional profile alongside them.

## 8. Rating Procedure

1. Define the interaction unit and user-directed project.
2. Read the full relevant context before rating individual turns.
3. Code observable events using the Interaction Annotation Schema.
4. Rate each dimension using the minimum evidence required by its definition.
5. Include a short evidence note for each score.
6. Use `NA` rather than guessing when evidence is insufficient.
7. Record disagreement between annotators rather than forcing premature consensus.

## 10. Candidate Validation Program

Future validation should include:

- expert review of construct definitions
- pilot coding across multiple model architectures
- inter-rater reliability testing
- comparison with ordinary search, editing, and static tool use
- convergent and discriminant validity studies
- longitudinal assessment of stability and change
- sensitivity to successful correction and induced drift
- testing across writing, programming, design, research, and decision tasks
- evaluation with participants of different experience levels

## 11. Interpretive Limits

The index is a research proposal, not a diagnostic test. Scores should not be used to make claims about a user’s mental health, dependency, intelligence, or capacity.

High loop health does not prove model understanding, consciousness, or human–AI fusion. Low loop health may reflect poor task fit, missing context, interface limitations, model failure, or insufficient evidence rather than a stable property of either participant.
